Buddy for the Blind

An IBM 
Corporate Social Responsibility project

As part of the IBM Social Resposibility innitiative, I came in contact with a school for blind children. During our first introductory meeting, we discussed a challenge they were facing with learning blind kids how to swim. Fear of hitting the pool-wall forces blind children to swim with one arm outstretched or raised, making progress slow. And even those children that could already swim, could not do so independently when swimming ‘at-speed’. A coach or trainer has to be with them constantly to prevent injury by hitting the wall, putting these children at a disadvantage when swimming. 

I started dreaming about a solution that was both easy enough to install without technical knowledge, that could be deployed without prohibitive costs, and on top was reliable enough to earn the trust of the kids. 

A small team - one developer, one UX Designer and me - started to work...

Hill Statement

Sandra, a young blind swimmer that loves to be in the swimming pool

can swim and turn by herself

without any assistance from a coach or person


To quickly convey the idea of the solution, we used the Hill Statement from the IBM Design Thinking methodology. By conducting user interviews, with a structured questionnaire, we created a Hill Statement accurately describing the to-be end state of the solution. These interviews were filmed and taped, thus allowing us to capture student-sentiment and make sure we were buidling what was wished for. 

Proposed solution

Blind Swimmers Pool Warning System

The result of the previous design thinking sessions was to build a warning system that could warn blind swimmers when they approached a wall. The proposed solution was based on a smart watch that a swimmer could wear on their wrist or under a headband combined with bluetooth beacons that could serve as ‘location anchors’.

Prototyping

Prototype 1

One beacon at each end of the pool

The first prototype I designed was based on one solitairy beacon at each end of the swimming lane. The watch would calculate the RSSI of those two beacons. Appraching  one beacon, the measured RSSI signal would cross a predetermined thresshold and trigger the watch to buzz. 

Prototype 2

Three beacons at each end of the pool

The second prototype consisted of three beacons on each end of the pool to be able to better detect reflections and filter out inaccurate RSSI measurements. These wrong measurements would lead to false positive or false negative alerts on the watch, both causing faith in the solution to drop. The idea was that wirth three beacons, the calculated average RSSI reading of each beacon would average out wrong calculations.

Prototype 3

A string of beacons over the pool

The last prototype I build consisted of a string of beacons mounted either a meter over the swimming lane or on the dividers between two swiming lanes. To calculate the position of the swimmer in the swimming lane, I developed a mathematical model that would create a rolling average of each beacon RSSI detecte. The model would output a slope-angle. Based on that slope angle, a swimmer would me either before, on top, or after the virtual tripwire. 

Mathematical Model

Slope Positive

This state means that the swimmer is still before the virtual tripwire. As long as the watch can calculate the slope, meaning enough beacons are being received, the watch buzzes at a 0.5hz frequency. 

Slope Horizontal

This state signals that the swimmer is crossing the virtual tripwire. As soon as the slope of all calculated mean RSSI’s is horizontal, the swimmer is crossing the tripwire and the watch starts to buzz at a 1hz frequency.

Slope Negative

When a slope-negative state is calculated, the swimmer is over the virtual tripwire. to signal the approaching wall and warm the swimmer, the watch start constant buzzing. At the same time, an alert is send to the coach in the pool to keep an eye out.

Beacon Design

Designing beacons for accurate location determination

For the solution to work, the beacons needed to send out their ID as frequently as possible. Standard iBeacon configuration defaults to 100ms between transmits. This was too low to reliably detact if a swimmer crossed the virtual tripwire. 

The beacons’ manufacturer Kontakt.io was willing to work with us on a custom firware that would allow for a 25ms transmission interval. This resulted in a reliable mathematical model to calculate a swimmers position relative to the beacons. 

To solve the higher power consumption, the beacons were installed in custom waterproof enclosures with AA Lithium batteries. The result was a net battery lifetime of one year. 

Mounting the beacons

Sketches and 3D designs

From previous trials it was determined that mounting the beacons either above the swimming lane or on the swimming-lane dividers would lead to the most predictable alarms.

These two design-sketches were made on a flight to Helsinki. They were later 3D printed to test in the pool.

Prototyping in the Pool

Trials by the project team

Although first tests were done from behind the desk, testing in-real-life is important. Water causes reflection in the Bluetooth RSSI reading, causing issues with the measured location of the swimmer. 

In the first video trials on the right, the beacons are mounted on the guard-rail seperating the swimlanes. The second video shows the beacons approx a meter above the water in the middle of the swimlane. 

Real life testing

Testing both mounting solutions

From previous trials it was determined that mounting the beacons either above the swimming lane or on the swimming-lane dividers would lead to the most predictable alarms.

These two design-sketches were made on a flight to Helsinki and were later 3D printed to test in the pool.

There were pro’s and cons to both locations, but above the swimlane prevailed. The main argument was that this solution worked independently from from the location in the swimming lane.
